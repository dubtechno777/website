Why timing is everything in reverb and delay

THE PROBLEM WITH GUESSING When reverb isn't synced to your track's BPM, it creates phase issues, muddies your low end, and makes your mix sound amateurish. The wrong timing forces elements to fight for space instead of working together.
HOW PROS THINK ABOUT TIME Think of your DAW as a time machine. Every element needs to occupy its own space in the timeline. Reverb and delay aren't just effects—they're time-based instruments that must lock into your project's grid just like your drums and melody.
THE SOLUTION: PRECISION This calculator eliminates the guesswork. Instead of wasting hours tweaking by ear, you get scientifically precise starting points that ensure your spatial effects enhance rather than conflict with your music. It's not about limiting creativity—it's about building on a solid foundation.

ESSENTIALS: BPM TO MS

What's the formula for BPM to MS?

The core formula is 60000 / BPM. This gives you the milliseconds for a quarter note. Our calculator handles all the divisions and multiples for you.

How to set pre-delay for a tight mix?

Keep your reverb tail from muddying the transients. For a standard 120 BPM track, start with a tight 20-40ms to blend the reverb with the source. Increase to 1/16th or 1/8th note values (use the calculator) for a more pronounced, rhythmic effect.

How to sync hardware delays or older plugins?

Not all gear has BPM sync. Use the MS value from this tool and manually input it into the delay time parameter. It's the universal solution.

My delay is set to 500ms. What's the BPM?

At 500ms, your delay is synced to 120 BPM for quarter notes. For eighth notes, it would be 60 BPM. Reverse calculations are built into the tool.
